Where’s the Productivity? Why AI Gains Are Hard to Measure

AI is everywhere, but productivity statistics don’t show it—yet. Understanding why requires careful thinking about measurement and adoption.

Generative AI seems transformative. Demos are impressive. Companies report productivity gains. Yet official productivity statistics remain stubbornly flat. This gap—between perceived and measured productivity—echoes earlier technology puzzles. Understanding why is essential for predicting whether AI will eventually deliver economic transformation.

The Solow Paradox

In 1987, economist Robert Solow famously observed: “You can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics.” For decades after mainframes and PCs spread through business, measured productivity growth remained low.

Then, in the late 1990s, productivity spiked. The returns arrived, just delayed. Explanations for the delay include:

  • Measurement lags: Statistics take time to capture new technologies
  • Adoption lags: Technology must diffuse through the economy
  • Complementary investments: Organizations must reorganize to exploit new technology
  • Learning curves: Workers must develop new skills

Are we in a similar Solow paradox moment with AI? The technology is visible, but the productivity gains haven’t yet materialized in aggregate data.

Measurement Problems

Productivity = output / labor hours. Simple in principle; fiendishly hard in practice.

Quality adjustment: If software is 10× better but costs the same, output should be counted as higher. But how do you measure “10× better”? Statistical agencies try to quality-adjust, but it’s imperfect and subjective.

New products: How do you measure the productivity of something that didn’t exist before? Google Search, smartphones, ChatGPT—these create value that traditional output measures struggle to capture.

Intangibles: Modern economies produce software, data, and organizational capability—not physical goods. These are hard to measure and often not counted as investment.

Free services: Many AI applications are free to users (search, translation, content moderation). They generate consumer surplus but show up as zero in output statistics.

Sector heterogeneity: Productivity might be soaring in tech and stagnant in healthcare. Aggregate statistics average over everything.

The honest answer: we don’t really know if AI is boosting productivity because we don’t have great tools to measure it.

Adoption J-Curves

Technology adoption typically follows a pattern:

  1. Early investment: Buy the technology, train workers, reorganize processes. This is costly.
  2. Productivity dip: Initially, output may fall as workers learn and systems are debugged.
  3. Productivity gains: Eventually, the technology pays off. But “eventually” can be years.

If you measured electricity’s impact in 1910, you’d see costs without benefits. Factories had to be rebuilt around electric motors (rather than central steam engines with belt drives). The productivity gains came in the 1920s.

We may be in the investment phase of AI adoption. Companies are spending billions on compute, tooling, and integration. The payoff comes later.

Complementary Investments

New technology rarely produces value in isolation. You need:

Worker skills: Employees must learn to use AI tools effectively. This takes training and experience. Prompt engineering, AI-assisted workflows, trust calibration—all require learning.

Process redesign: Existing processes were designed for humans without AI. To fully exploit AI, you need new processes. This requires management attention and organizational experimentation.

Data and infrastructure: AI systems require data pipelines, integration with existing systems, and appropriate governance. This is expensive and slow.

Cultural change: Organizations have to believe AI is worth using and trust it appropriately. This doesn’t happen overnight.

Until these complementary investments are made, AI sits in organizations without producing full value. This explains why companies can adopt AI without immediately seeing productivity gains.

Where Productivity Gains Are Visible

At the micro level, individual applications do show clear gains:

Coding: GitHub data suggests Copilot users complete tasks 55% faster (though they also accept much AI-generated code that may require later fixing). Other studies show smaller but real effects.

Customer service: Companies report 15-30% productivity improvements with LLM-assisted agents. More tickets resolved per agent-hour.

Writing and documentation: First-draft generation is dramatically faster. Editing AI output is faster than writing from scratch.

Translation: Machine translation has largely replaced human translation for many low-stakes applications.

Research assistance: Literature review, summarization, and brainstorming are accelerated.

These micro-level gains are real but may not yet show up in aggregate statistics due to limited diffusion and measurement issues.

General-Purpose Technology Theory

Some technologies are general-purpose technologies (GPTs)—broadly applicable innovations that transform many sectors over decades. Economists often cite three historical GPTs:

  1. Steam engine: Took ~50 years to fully transform manufacturing, transportation
  2. Electricity: Took ~30 years from adoption to productivity surge
  3. Computers/Internet: Took ~20 years; productivity boom in late 1990s

If AI is a GPT, we should expect: - Long diffusion period - Slow initial productivity gains - Eventual large, broad-based transformation

Whether AI is truly a GPT—and not just a narrow capability improvement—remains debated. Its breadth of application suggests it might be. But history isn’t prediction.

Baumol’s Cost Disease

William Baumol observed that some sectors resist productivity improvements. A string quartet takes the same time to play today as in 1800. Hospital stays require human attention. Education involves teacher-student interaction.

These labor-intensive services tend to see costs rise over time (as wages increase with general productivity) without corresponding output gains. This is Baumol’s cost disease.

AI might attack precisely these resistant sectors: - Healthcare: diagnostic assistance, administrative automation - Education: tutoring, personalized instruction - Government: form processing, case management

If AI cures Baumol’s cost disease in these sectors, the productivity effects would be large. But these sectors are also heavily regulated and culturally resistant to change. The potential is there; whether it’s realized is uncertain.

What to Expect

Based on historical patterns and current evidence, my expectations:

Short term (1-3 years): Visible improvements in specific applications. Aggregate productivity statistics remain flat or modestly up. Measurement challenges obscure effects.

Medium term (3-10 years): Broader adoption as complementary investments mature. Process redesign catches up. Productivity gains begin appearing in official statistics. Uneven distribution across sectors.

Long term (10+ years): If AI is a true GPT, potentially transformative effects comparable to electricity or computers. New industries, new products, reshaped labor markets. But this is speculative.

Leading indicators: Watch for: - Rising firm-level productivity correlating with AI adoption - Labor reallocation (workers shifting between tasks and industries) - New product categories enabled by AI - Process patents and organizational innovations

Why This Matters

The productivity question isn’t academic. If AI delivers large productivity gains: - Economic growth accelerates - Living standards rise - Some labor displacement occurs but new opportunities emerge

If AI doesn’t deliver—if it’s expensive hype without substance: - Massive investment turns out to be misallocated - AI companies disappoint - Expectations reset painfully

The truth is probably in between. AI will deliver real value, but unevenly, slowly, and with measurement difficulties that make the gains hard to see until they’ve already arrived.

For individuals and organizations, the implication is clear: don’t wait for aggregate statistics to tell you what to do. The micro-level gains are already real enough to act on. The macro-level measurement will catch up eventually.
